What is the difference between structured content and semantic HTML?

Structured content frames its content as packages made up of small bits of data that have a defined relation to each other rather than just presenting a block of text. Semantic HTML is a markup text that communicates the relationship between different document elements.

How do they work together?

They work together to provide meaning and structure to the content on the website, and also allows people and technology to recognize what each elements are supposed to mean.

Why is it important to have structured content and semantic HTML in your web pages?

It's important to have both in the web pages as it provides its structure and meaning. For example, without semantic HTML, it could confused assistive technology such as screen readers. It also communicates hierarcy and the different relationships between different elements properly.

Denise Jacobs argues that we all have a troll-like inner critic. Of the methods she identifies to banish this troll, which would be most successful for you? Why?

One method Jacobs suggests to banish the inner critic is to aim for "good" instead of "perfect". I personally also think this works best for me because I am unfortunately a perfectionist and I have to constantly remind myself that whatever I get done, nothing can ever be done perfectly and there will always be flaws.

In David McRaney's article, what is "hyperbolic discounting"? List one method McRaney identifies to counteract hyperbolic discounting. Which would work best for you?

According to David McRaney's article, the tendency to get more rational when you are forced to wait is called hyperbolic discounting. Furthermore, McRaney states that one way to counter hyperbolic discounting is through what is known as "precommitment", which is essentially setting mini deadlines instead of one big final one. For me, that exact method helps me stay on top of my workload the best because it keeps me accountable and prevents any procrastination from taking place.
